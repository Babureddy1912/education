The active and passive voices

Most sentences have a subject, a verb and an object.

The parents (subject) liked (verb) the school (object).

  • The subject is the person, group or thing doing the action: ‘The parents’.
  • The verb is the action itself: ‘liked’.
  • The object is the person, group or thing that the action is done to: ‘the school’.

Even if the sentence is longer, the subject, verb and object remain in the same order:

The parents, who were visiting for the first time, liked the school.

In both these examples, ‘liked’ is an active verb.

Many verbs can be active or passive. When the verb is passive, the subject is on the receiving end of the action:

The school was liked by the parents.

subject

verb

Here, the reader learns what is liked before learning who likes it. The doer (or agent) is identified using ‘by’: ‘by the parents’.

However, often in passive sentences the agent is not identified, for example: ‘The school was well liked.’ All passive forms are made up using the verb ‘be’ + the past participle:

The inspector will next consider the pupils’ behaviour. The pupils’ behaviour will be considered next by the Inspector.

The headteacher ended the meeting abruptly. The meeting was ended abruptly by the headteacher.

  1. 1

    Change these active sentences into passive ones. Remember to adjust the wording:

    1. The inspector visited Mr Kerry's classroom twice that morning.
    2. Regular year assemblies create a strong sense of community.
    3. Pupils regularly assess their personal progress in every subject.

  2. 2

    Change these passive sentences into active ones:

    1. The school is driven forward by challenging and ambitious teachers.
    2. The needs of all pupils are taken into consideration through careful timetabling.
    3. Visits to classrooms are made regularly by the headteacher.

  3. 3

    Rewrite the following in the passive voice:

    The student noticed the fire. Immediately, he struck the fire alarm. His quick action saved many lives that day.
